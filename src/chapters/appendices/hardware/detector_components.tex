\section{Detector Components}
\label{sec:appendix-detector-components}

This appendix records supplementary detector descriptions, component
specifications, mechanical constraints, and hardware inventories.

\subsection{LGAD Sensor Technology}
\label{sec:appendix-lgad-technology}

Low-gain avalanche detectors are silicon sensors designed to provide moderate
internal charge multiplication while preserving fast timing performance. In a
standard LGAD, a highly doped gain layer below the junction creates a localized
high-field region. Charge carriers produced by an ionizing particle drift
through this region and undergo controlled impact ionization, increasing the
signal size without operating the device in Geiger mode. The resulting pulses
are fast enough for precision time measurements and large enough to improve
the signal-to-noise ratio for thin sensors
\cite{Pellegrini2014LGAD,Giacomini2023LGADReview}.

\begin{center}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.42\textwidth]{../resources/figures/appendicies/hardware/lgad_thinned_size}
  \captionsetup{hypcap=false}
  \captionof{figure}{Photograph of a thinned LGAD sensor developed for the ATAR R\&D
  program. Reducing sensor thickness decreases material budget while preserving
  precise timing performance.}
  \label{fig:appendix-lgad-thinned-sensor}
\end{center}

For an active target, the relevant LGAD advantages are thin active material,
fast charge collection, and timing at the \(\order{\withunit{100}{\pico\second}}\)
scale for minimum-ionizing particles. These features are well matched to the
PIONEER requirement to measure a pion stop, a short low-energy muon segment,
and an outgoing positron inside the same compact detector volume. The main
technology tradeoff is spatial segmentation. Conventional segmented LGADs can
lose efficiency near inter-pad gaps, while AC-coupled LGADs spread the signal
capacitively across readout electrodes and can improve fill factor and spatial
interpolation. AC-LGAD design therefore involves optimizing gain, electrode
pitch, resistive layer properties, capacitance, timing, and charge sharing for
the intended readout geometry \cite{Kita2022ACLGAD,Giacomini2023LGADReview}.

\subsection{Tracker R\&D Concepts}
\label{sec:appendix-tracker-rd}

The tracker design is still an R\&D topic, and the details are expected to
evolve with simulations, trigger studies, and prototype measurements. The
original detector concept used a cylindrical tracker based on micro-resistive
WELL (\(\mu\)-RWELL) technology. A \(\mu\)-RWELL detector is a
micro-pattern gaseous detector (MPGD) in which gas amplification occurs in a
patterned amplification stage coupled through a resistive layer to the
readout. Later geometry studies considered alternatives, including compact
``bullet''-like or hemispherical tracker volumes surrounding the ATAR while
respecting the calorimeter and beamline constraints
\cite{pioneer_tracker_geometries,tracker_toward_pioneer_2025}.

Current hardware studies include MPGD concepts such as \(\mu\)RGroove
prototypes. Prototype detectors are being characterized with x rays to measure
gain and gain uniformity, and available prototypes use different readout
schemes, including \(xy\) and \(uv\) strip geometries. If an MPGD tracker is
adopted, a \(uv\) readout scheme is attractive because it can keep the
front-end electronics upstream of the positron acceptance. Planned test-beam
measurements are intended to compare readout schemes and measure rate
capability, timing resolution, and spatial resolution.

Representative tracker requirements under discussion include hit resolution
below \(\withunit{1}{\milli\meter}\), timing below
\(\withunit{1}{\nano\second}\), efficiency above
\(\withunit{99.5}{\percent}\) for two planes, low edge material budget, and a
channel count compatible with trigger-path digitization. These numbers should
be interpreted as design targets for ongoing optimization rather than final
detector specifications \cite{atar_kpp_2025,tracker_toward_pioneer_2025}.

\subsection{Liquid-Xenon Backup Calorimeter Concept}
\label{sec:appendix-lxe-calorimeter}

Liquid xenon is retained as an official backup calorimeter technology for
PIONEER. Its role in the experiment is the same as the LYSO calorimeter: to
measure positron energy and timing with high efficiency, provide a stable
high-energy region for the \(\pi\to e\nu\) sample, and control the response
tail that feeds events across the analysis threshold. LXe is attractive
because it is a homogeneous scintillating medium rather than an array of
separate crystals. The absence of internal crystal boundaries reduces
sampling nonuniformities, and the short radiation length and small Moliere
radius allow a compact detector with substantial shower containment. LXe also
has high scintillation yield, about \(\withunit{46}{VUV\ photons\per\keV}\),
and fast scintillation components, making it a credible technology for the
energy, timing, and pileup requirements of the Phase-I \(R_{e/\mu}\)
measurement. The current concept uses a fiducial angular coverage near
\(\withunit{120}{\degree}\), with an LXe volume sized to provide shower
containment comparable to the baseline calorimeter requirements
\cite{malbrunot_lxe_overview_2025}.

\begin{center}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{../resources/figures/appendicies/hardware/lxe_3d_render}
  \captionsetup{hypcap=false}
  \captionof{figure}{Conceptual rendering of a liquid-xenon calorimeter option for
  PIONEER. The LXe concept provides a homogeneous electromagnetic calorimeter
  around the target, with engineering studies focused on acceptance,
  photosensor material, timing, and response tails.}
  \label{fig:appendix-lxe-render}
\end{center}

The strongest experimental precedent is the MEG liquid-xenon calorimeter
program, which demonstrated percent-level electromagnetic energy resolution in
the same broad energy regime relevant for stopped-pion measurements. In a PIONEER implementation, current studies focus
on whether an LXe volume can meet the response-tail and timing requirements
while preserving high acceptance around the ATAR and beam entrance. A large
prototype with roughly \(19X_{0}\) depth and \(5\) Moliere radii is under
study to test whether the response tail and energy resolution are comparable
to the full calorimeter in the forward direction and to measure effects such
as photonuclear contributions to the lineshape
\cite{malbrunot_lxe_overview_2025,suzuki_lxe_cof_mppc_2025}.

The design challenges differ from those of LYSO. LXe requires cryogenic
operation, ultraviolet-sensitive photosensors, optical windows or sensor
packages with low material budget in the positron path, and a strategy for
pileup separation in a detector that is not naturally segmented into crystals.
The readout scale is also substantially larger than for the LYSO option: a
representative LXe design uses fewer than 2000 readout channels, including
roughly 1250 MPPC channels and 700 PMT channels, compared with about 400
channels for the LYSO calorimeter. This increases the raw data rate and makes
waveform handling, synchronization, and online reduction more important
design considerations. The photosensor packaging is therefore a central part
of the R\&D program: low-mass cold front-end and MPPC concepts are being
studied to reduce inactive material while maintaining light collection and
timing performance. Current studies also focus on inner photosensor
performance for pileup identification, position reconstruction at the
millimeter scale, angle-dependent photon-detection efficiency, diffuse
reflection, and possible MPPC aging under VUV exposure in LXe. The LXe option
is consequently evaluated through the same physics requirements as LYSO--energy
response, tail fraction, timing, acceptance, and pileup separation--but with a
different set of engineering and data-acquisition constraints
\cite{malbrunot_lxe_overview_2025,suzuki_lxe_cof_mppc_2025}.
